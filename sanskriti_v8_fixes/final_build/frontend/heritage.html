Bihar · 2500 Years Old

Madhubani Art

Madhubani painting is a 2,500-year-old folk art from the Mithila region of Bihar. Legend says it was first created for the wedding of Lord Ram and Sita. Today it is one of India's most recognised folk art forms, made using natural colours — turmeric, indigo, rice powder — with no empty space left on the canvas.

🎨 Natural Colours
Made from turmeric, indigo, rice powder. No synthetic dyes in authentic work.
📐 No Empty Space
Bold outlines and vivid geometric patterns fill every inch of the canvas.
🌸 Five Styles
Katchni, Bharni, Godna, Kohbar, Tantrik — each from a different village tradition.
🏅 GI Certified
Officially recognised as Geographical Indication by the Government of India.

Kashmir · 3000 Years Old

Pashmina

Pashmina comes from the underbelly hair of the Changthangi goat found at 14,000 feet in Ladakh. Hand-spun by Kashmiri artisans into the world's finest wool. A single shawl takes 3–6 months. Genuine Pashmina passes the ring test and burns like hair, never plastic.

💍 Ring Test
Genuine Pashmina passes through a standard ring due to its fineness.
⏱️ 3-6 Months
A single hand-woven shawl takes months of dedicated craftsmanship.
🪡 Sozni Embroidery
Delicate hand embroidery added that can take years for elaborate pieces.
🏅 GI Certified
Officially protected Geographical Indication from Kashmir.

Maharashtra · Tribal Art

Warli Painting

Warli is India's oldest living folk art from the Warli tribe of Maharashtra. White rice-paste figures on a terracotta background tell stories using only three shapes — circle, triangle, square. Traditionally painted exclusively by women for weddings and harvest festivals.

⚪ White on Brown
Only white rice paste on terracotta background — no other colours used.
📐 Three Shapes
Circle, triangle, square — complex scenes built from these basic forms.

Rajasthan & Gujarat · 5000 Years Old

Bandhani

Bandhani — meaning "to tie" — is one of the world's oldest tie-dye traditions, with evidence from the Indus Valley Civilisation. A single saree can have over 100,000 hand-tied dots, each tied with a fingernail before dyeing with natural colours.

Banarasi Silk
