Bihar · 2500 Years Old
🖼️

Madhubani Art

Madhubani painting is a 2,500-year-old folk art from the Mithila region of Bihar. Legend says it was first created for the wedding of Lord Ram and Sita — the women of Mithila decorated every wall and floor of the kingdom. Today, it's one of India's most recognised folk art forms, practised primarily by women who pass the tradition from mother to daughter.

"When a daughter is born in Mithila, the women paint the walls. When she marries, they paint again. Madhubani is the language of our hearts."

Kashmir · 3000 Years Old
🧣

Pashmina

Pashmina comes from the underbelly hair of the Changthangi goat found only in the high-altitude Changthang plateau of Ladakh at 14,000 feet. The fibres are hand-spun by Kashmiri artisans into the world's finest wool — softer than cashmere, lighter than air. A single shawl takes 3–6 months to make by hand.

How to identify genuine Pashmina
  • Burns like hair (not plastic smell)
  • Ring test — passes through a ring
  • GI certification tag from Kashmir
  • Hand-spun, not machine-woven
Shop Pashmina
How Pashmina is Made
1
Combing — Fibres are hand-combed from the Changthangi goat during spring moulting season
2
Spinning — Village women hand-spin the raw fibre into thread using a traditional yinder (spinning wheel)
3
Dyeing — Threads are dyed with plant-based or mineral colours in Kashmir's mountain streams
4
Weaving — Master weavers hand-loom the shawl on traditional khaddi looms, one inch per hour
5
Finishing — Kani or Sozni embroidery is added by hand. A Kani shawl can take 2–3 years
Rajasthan & Gujarat · 5000 Years Old
🎨

Bandhani

Bandhani (from Sanskrit bandhan, meaning "to tie") is one of the oldest tie-dye traditions in the world. Evidence has been found in the Indus Valley Civilisation. Artisans use their fingernails to pluck thousands of tiny dots of fabric and tie them with thread before dyeing — creating the iconic polka-dot and geometric patterns. A single saree can have over 100,000 tie points.

Jaipur, Rajasthan

Blue Pottery

Jaipur Blue Pottery is unique — it contains no clay. The base is made from quartz stone powder, glass, and multani mitti (Fuller's Earth) fired at low temperature. The Persian-inspired cobalt blue designs on a milky white background make each piece unmistakable. It's fragile, artistic, and impossible to mass-produce — every piece is individually hand-painted.

Shop Blue Pottery
What Makes It Special
✦
No clay used — Made from quartz, making it technically not "pottery" at all
✦
Persian influence — Brought to Rajasthan by Mughal artisans in the 14th century
✦
Two firings — Fired at low 800°C twice for the signature glassy finish
✦
GI certified — Only Jaipur-made pieces can legally be called Blue Pottery

Warli Painting

Warli is a tribal art from the Warli tribe of Maharashtra, India's oldest living folk art. Created exclusively in white (rice paste) on a mud-brown background, it tells stories of daily life — harvest festivals, weddings, fishing, and dancing. The geometric vocabulary uses only circles, triangles, and squares to create complex narrative scenes.

Shop Warli Art
⚪
Only White on Brown

Authentic Warli uses only white rice paste on a terracotta or mud background — no other colours.

📐
Three Shapes Only

Circle (sun/moon), triangle (human body), square (sacred enclosure). Complex scenes from 3 shapes.

💃
Tarpa Dance

The most iconic Warli motif — people dancing in a circle around a musician. Symbol of community.

👩
Women's Art

Traditionally painted exclusively by women for weddings and festivals. Men began painting in the 1970s.

Karnataka · 200 Years Old
🪀

Channapatna Toys

Channapatna is called Gombegala Ooru — "town of toys" in Kannada. Established 200 years ago under Tipu Sultan who invited Persian craftsmen, these wooden toys are made from ivory wood (Wrightia tinctoria) and finished with natural lacquer made from vegetable dyes. They are completely non-toxic, eco-friendly, and safe for children. GI protected.

Shop Channapatna Toys
Making Process
1
Wood selection — Ivory wood (aale mara) is chosen for its soft, non-toxic properties
2
Lathe turning — Wood is turned on a traditional lathe to create the round toy shapes
3
Lac application — Natural lac is pressed against the rotating wood, melting to form a colourful coating
4
Polishing — Final buff with a soft cloth gives the characteristic smooth, glowing finish

Banarasi Silk

Banarasi silk sarees are woven in Varanasi (Banaras), one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities. The weaving tradition dates to the Mughal era. They are famous for their gold and silver brocade or zari work, fine silk, and opulent embroidery. Every Indian bride dreams of a Banarasi saree — it's the ultimate symbol of elegance and heritage.

West Bengal & Odisha · 4000 Years Old
🔔

Dokra Metal Casting

Dokra is one of the world's oldest metal-casting techniques, using the lost-wax (cire-perdue) method used for over 4,000 years. The Dancing Girl of Mohenjodaro (2500 BCE) is believed to be the earliest known Dokra work. Tribal artisans from the Dhokra Damar communities of Bengal and Odisha create one-of-a-kind brass figurines, animals, and jewellery — no two pieces are identical.

Shop Dokra Art
Lost-Wax Process (Cire Perdue)
1
Clay core — A basic shape is formed in clay and dried in the sun
2
Wax coating — The clay core is coated in beeswax and resin, then carved with all the fine details
3
Outer clay — The wax model is coated in a thick clay shell and dried
4
Metal pour — Molten brass is poured in, melting and replacing the wax (hence "lost-wax")
5
Reveal — The clay is broken away, revealing a unique brass sculpture that can never be exactly replicated
